Introduction

Web browsers have been a cornerstone in how people access the internet for decades. From their earliest days to the modern multi-tabbed, extension-enabled experiences, browsers have continually evolved alongside web standards. They shape how we view, create, and interact with online content.

Over time, browsers have become more secure, more standards-compliant, and have expanded to include tools for developers, advanced privacy options, and integrations with cloud services. It’s hard to imagine modern life without these powerful applications.

If you'd like to learn more about early web technologies, feel free to visit the W3C website.

A brief history of IE

Internet Explorer (IE) was introduced by Microsoft in 1995, quickly becoming one of the most widely used browsers due to its bundling with Windows. Over the years, IE received numerous updates, though it sometimes lagged in adopting new web standards. Despite declining usage in later years, its early dominance played a key role in popularizing the web.

After many versions, Microsoft officially ended support for older IE versions and transitioned users to Microsoft Edge. While IE’s market share has dramatically decreased, it remains a significant part of browser history.

To explore more about Microsoft’s current browser developments, visit the Microsoft Edge page.

A brief history of Firefox

Firefox, developed by the Mozilla Foundation, was first released in 2002 under the name “Phoenix.” It quickly became popular thanks to its open-source nature, focus on privacy, and commitment to web standards. Over time, Firefox introduced pioneering features such as tabbed browsing and robust add-ons, influencing the broader browser market.

Despite facing stiff competition from other browsers, Firefox continues to have a dedicated user base. Mozilla’s emphasis on an open and accessible internet remains at the core of Firefox’s identity.

You can learn more about Mozilla’s mission at Mozilla’s official website.

A brief history of Chrome

Google Chrome was launched in 2008 and rapidly gained market share, becoming one of the most widely used browsers in the world. Its speed, minimalist design, and integration with Google services appealed to users, and the frequent updates kept Chrome on the cutting edge of web technology.

Over time, Chrome extended its platform to include powerful developer tools, a vast library of extensions, and cross-device synchronization. Its dominance has influenced modern web standards and shaped the competitive landscape of browser development.

For more information about Google’s browser innovations, visit the official Chrome page.
